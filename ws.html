Introduction (Chromatography)

Chromatography is an essential analytical technique that enables the separation and identification of components within complex mixtures. This experiment explores the fundamental principles of chromatographic separation, focusing on the differing affinities of substances for stationary and mobile phases. A key aspect of accurately interpreting chromatographic data is the ability to balance chemical equations, ensuring that the number of atoms for each element remains consistent on both the reactant and product sides, in accordance with the law of conservation of mass. This skill is vital when preparing reagents, analyzing reaction products, and ensuring the precision of quantitative results obtained through chromatography.

This experiment provides practical experience in performing chromatography and develops the skills necessary to interpret chromatograms and balance chemical equations. By identifying individual components within a mixture, a deeper understanding of chromatographic techniques and their significant applications in scientific research and industry, from pharmaceuticals to environmental analysis, will be achieved.

Discussion (Chromatography)

In the paper chromatography experiment, different pigments from the color-coated candy were identified and separated. The results revealed four distinct colors: the orange pigment, with an Rf value of 0.36, showed the highest affinity to the solvent; the yellow pigment had an Rf value of 0.32, and the blue pigment had an Rf value of 0.3, both indicating moderate affinity; while the red pigment, with an Rf value of 0.4, indicated low affinity, traveling the fastest and exhibiting less interaction with the stationary phase. Challenges were encountered during the experiment, including inconsistent sample applications, which could have affected the Rf calculations.

In the balancing experiment, the focus was on the process of balancing chemical equations in accordance with the law of conservation of mass. This principle states that matter cannot be created or destroyed, meaning that the number of atoms must remain equal on both sides of the equation. Several equations were balanced to deepen understanding of this concept. It was observed that each balanced equation maintained the same number of atoms. Conclusion (Molecular Geometry)

The Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion (VSEPR) provides a basic understanding of molecular geometry and how it affects the behavior of chemicals. The theory emphasized how important electron pair repulsion is in determining how atoms are arranged within a molecule. The molecule’s shapes and interactions with other molecules are therefore determined by the arrangement.

The VSEPR theory's application goes beyond molecular geometry predictions because it provides insights on how molecules' polarity affects their solubility, boiling point, and reactivity. The VSEPR theory also offers a structure for understanding how different molecules interact with pollutants in water filtration. A molecule’s ability to trap particular pollutants is influenced by its shape, size, and polarity, which are all determined by the arrangement of its electron pairs.

Furthermore, bonded and lone pairs of electrons will align around the center of an atom in order to maximize their distances from one another and reduce electron repulsion. Bond angles and molecule structure are affected because lone pairs have a more stronger repulsive force than bonding pairs because of their higher electron number

Applications and Real-Life Connections (Chromatography)

The following are the applications and real-life connections of paper chromatography:


  • Isolating Substances: Paper chromatography can effectively isolate both known and unknown substances, allowing for detailed analysis.
  • Identifying Pure Substances: This technique is valuable in distinguishing pure substances from mixtures, ensuring quality control in various industries.
  • Determining Decomposition Products: It is used to analyze the products of decomposition, providing insights into chemical reactions.
  • Forensic Science: In forensic investigations, paper chromatography identifies components of inks, dyes, and other substances found at crime scenes, aiding in criminal investigations.
  • Pharmaceuticals: The method is crucial for analyzing drug purity and identifying potential contaminants, ensuring safety and efficacy in medications.
  • Food Science: It helps analyze food composition, identify artificial colors, and detect adulteration, ensuring food safety and quality.

  • Stoichiometry Calculations: Balancing chemical equations is essential for stoichiometry, helping to understand the relationships between reactants and products in a chemical reaction.
  • Predicting Reaction Products: A balanced equation can be used to predict the products of a chemical reaction, aiding in experimental planning.
  • Construction Safety: In construction, balancing is crucial when using explosives, such as dynamite, to ensure the correct amount is applied to safely demolish a building without causing unnecessary damage.
  • Medication Dosing: Accurate balancing is essential in medicine, where doctors calculate the appropriate dosage based on a patient’s body weight to prevent overdosing or underdosing, ensuring effective treatment
